Why are China's electric arc furnaces struggling?

  • August 14, 2020

Why are China's electric arc furnaces struggling?

Chinas scrap-intensive electric arc furnaces (EAF) capacity utilization rates surged from a record low of 10% during COVID-19 afflicted February to a recent reading of 58% in early August.

Over the same period, those Chinese EAFs that consume both hot metal and scrap witnessed a rebound from a modest low of 61% back up to 70% capacity utilization.

One major advantage of the scrap-fed electric arc furnaces steelmaking process is the ability to switch off production in response to a material downturn in steel consumption and margins.

That said, their major disadvantages have been the exposure to inflated ferrous scrap and temporary volatility in ultra-high-power graphite electrode costs over the past three years.

We calculate that Chinese scrap-fed EAF rebar producers suffered a loss of RMB270/t compared with BF-BOF profit margins of RMB184/t at the height of COVID-19 lockdown measures in February.

Diverging steelmaking economics between processes

Despite resurgent iron ore and stubbornly high metallurgical coke prices, Chinese blast furnace-basic oxygen (BF-BOF) steelmakers have maintained a relatively healthy degree of profitability compared with their EAF-based peers.

More recently, Navigate Commodities calculates Tangshan BF-BOF rebar margins stood at RMB234/t (incl. VAT) in mid-August compared with RMB45/t for BF-EAF and an RMB26/t for scrap-intensive EAFs on the same basis.

Since Q2 2017, Chinese integrated steel producers significantly increased their ferrous-scrap usage in basic oxygen steelmaking due to induction furnace closures which created a temporary, but substantial reservoir (c.50-70Mt) of relatively cheap iron units.

While Chinas technical BOF scrap usage rates have not yet peaked, they reached an economic tolerance of 189/kg per tonne of liquid steel (kg/tls) in 2019, from 157kt/tls the previous year, given the trade-off between availability and cost competitiveness.

More recently, Tangshan (Chinas key steelmaking hub) blast furnace capacity utilization rates have soared to 88% in early August from the recent low of 69% at the end of February in response to the post-COVID-19 V-shaped recovery in steel consumption.

As a result, material increases in BOF output and relative scrap use, given its sheer scale, has created a significant tightness in ferrous scrap supply and inflated domestic prices, disadvantaging those local scrap-dependent electric arc furnaces.

Is there any room for Chinese EAFs to manoeuvre?

With Chinese BF-BOFs running at close to full capacity utilization, the scope of further ferrous scrap consumption growth will be relatively limited via this steelmaking process.

Moreover, we expect robust basic oxygen furnace steel production and underlying scrap consumption will keep domestic ferrous scrap prices relatively high.

Based on current market fundamentals, we believe that Tangshan ferrous scrap benchmarks have almost peaked at RMB2,915/t (incl. VAT) while any downside risk is unlikely.

Therefore, Chinese scrap-intensive electric arc furnaces will require a marked improvement in domestic rebar consumption, prices, and margins to support any increase in output.

We forecast that this base-case scenario will likely eventuate once heavy rains and resulting floods subside, enabling stimulus-driven construction steel consumption growth to resume.

The highest risk to the competitiveness Chinese scrap-fed EAFs versus BF-BOFs is our medium-term outlook for weaker iron ore prices, which will significantly lower hot metal production costs.

Those hybrid electric arc furnaces able to consume a mixture of hot metal and scrap will likely alter their charge towards molten iron-use to improve their cost competitiveness.

We do not foresee any material increase in domestic ferrous scrap generation in the short to medium term, as China carries out its long-run development of the recycling industry.

One potential release valve for EAFs would be the reclassification and removal of low ferrous scrap import quotas, permitting some mills to subsidize high input costs with highly competitive imports.
